APOL1 and Its Origin

Everyone has a gene called APOL1 or apolipoprotein L1. We each have two copies of the APOL1 gene, one inherited from each of our parents.

Some people have changes in the APOL1 gene, known as variants or mutations, which increase their risk of developing chronic kidney disease.

The APOL1 gene variants originally evolved in people who lived in Western and Central Africa, to protect them from a deadly disease known as African sleeping sickness. However, these same gene variants are now known to increase a person's risk of developing kidney disease.

APOL1 Kidney Disease

APOL1 kidney disease, also known as APOL1-mediated kidney disease (AMKD), refers to chronic kidney disease in patients with APOL1 gene variants and is linked to several types of kidney disorders.

APOL1 kidney disease is a genetic condition that people can inherit from their family because it is passed down through genes. APOL1 kidney disease impacts people globally.

Changes (mutations or variants) in the APOL1 gene can lead to kidney damage. Having the APOL1 gene variants can increase the chance of an individual losing kidney function faster.

Recognizing Symptoms

Kidney diseases can show up in different ways. Symptoms for APOL1 Kidney Disease are similar to other kidney diseases. Some people may have one or more signs or symptoms that something is wrong, but others may not have any until their kidney disease is very severe. That is why it is important to see a doctor and check their kidney function with simple urine and blood tests.

You, Your Family and the APOL1 Gene

APOL1 genetic testing provides valuable information about your health, your family's health, available treatment options, and potential eligibility for clinical trials.

Maze’s Commitment to You

APOL1 Kidney Disease currently has no approved treatments. To address this unmet need, Maze is developing a new oral medication called MZE829 as a potential therapy for APOL1 kidney disease.

Along with the help of trusted patient advisors, physicians, and scientists, Maze has launched our Phase 2 Clinical Trial for APOL1 Kidney Disease. We are currently looking for individuals to join our trial. For more information, please visit [ClinicalTrials.gov Page]

Maze Patient Advisory Committee

Maze is proud to partner with leaders and allies from the APOL1 kidney disease and Black chronic kidney disease (CKD) communities to create a Patient Advisory Committee for our APOL1 Kidney Disease Program. This committee ensures that the voices of patients are at the center of everything we do. By working together, we focus on making sure our clinical trials are patient centric, and support study access and participation.

APOL1 variants in kidney transplantation outcomes (2011 – present)

    • Kidneys from donors with two APOL1 risk alleles have a higher risk of graft failure post-transplantation, leading to more cautious donor selection processes.
    • Key References:
      • Reeves-Daniel A. M. (2011). “The APOL1 gene and allograft survival after kidney transplantation.” American Journal of Transplantation.

APOL1 variants in clinical disease studies (2011 – 2013)

  • Individuals with two APOL1 risk variants (G1/G1, G1/G2, or G2/G2) are at a significantly increased risk of developing FSGS, HIVAN, hypertensive nephrosclerosis, and other kidney diseases.
  • In individuals with two APOL1 risk alleles, kidney disease tends to progress more rapidly to ESRD, which explains the higher rates of kidney failure in African American populations.
  • Key References:
    • Kopp J. B. (2011). “APOL1 genetic variants in focal segmental glomerulosclerosis and HIV-associated nephropathy.” Journal of the American Society of Nephrology.
    • Parsa A. (2013). “APOL1 risk variants, race, and progression of chronic kidney disease.” The New England Journal of Medicine.

Discovery of APOL1 genetic variants and kidney disease (2010)

  • APOL1 variants, known as G1 and G2, are strongly associated with an increased risk of developing chronic kidney disease (CKD). These variants are more common in people of African descent and may explain higher rates of kidney disease in this population.
  • Key References:
    • Genovese G. (2010). “Association of trypanolytic ApoL1 variants with kidney disease in African Americans.” Science.

APOL1 and trypanosome resistance (2003)

  • APOL1 was found to have a critical role in innate immunity by conferring resistance to Trypanosoma brucei, the parasite responsible for African sleeping sickness. The APOL1 protein causes lysis of the parasite, which protects individuals from infection.
  • Key References:
    • Vanhamme L., (2003). “Apolipoprotein L-I is the trypanosome lytic factor of human serum.” Nature.
Read more
APOL1 gene and protein discovery (1997)

  • The APOL1 gene was identified as part of the apolipoprotein L gene family. This early discovery highlighted APOL1 as part of the high-density lipoprotein (HDL) complex.
  • Key References:
    • Duchateau, P. N. (1997). “Apolipoprotein L, a new human high-density lipoprotein apolipoprotein expressed by the pancreas.” The Journal of Biological Chemistry.

Pre-discovery period (1980s – 1900s)

  • Studies explored the resistance of certain African populations to African sleeping sickness, focusing on genetic factors that offered protection against Trypanosoma brucei.
  • Researchers hypothesized that blood serum factors in these populations had a role in lysing the parasite and preventing infection. However, the specific genes involved had not yet been identified.
  • Key References:
    • Rifkin M. R. (1978). “Trypanosoma brucei: some properties of the cytotoxic reaction induced by normal human serum.” Experimental Parasitology.

    • Tomlinson S. (1996). “The lysis of Trypanosoma brucei brucei by human serum.” Nature Biotechnology.
